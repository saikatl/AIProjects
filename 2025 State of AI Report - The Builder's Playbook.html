2025 State of AI Report

The Builder's Playbook by ICONIQ Capital, LLC

Introduction

The "2025 State of AI Report" by ICONIQ Capital, LLC, also known as "The Builder's Playbook," focuses on the practical aspects of conceiving, delivering, and scaling AI-powered offerings end-to-end. This report pivots from last year's focus on the buying journey and enterprise adoption to provide a "how-to" guide for architects, engineers, and product leaders in AI.

The report draws on proprietary survey results from April 2025, involving 300 executives at software companies building AI products, including CEOs, Heads of Engineering, Heads of AI, and Heads of Product. It also incorporates insights and best practices from AI leaders within the ICONIQ community.

Here are the key takeaways from the report, organized by core dimensions of the builder's playbook:

1. AI Product Roadmap & Architecture

AI-Native companies are advancing faster:

  • AI-native companies, whose core product or business model is AI-driven, are further along in their development cycle.
  • Approximately 47% of AI-native products have reached critical scale and proven market fit, compared to AI-enabled companies that embed AI features into existing offerings.
  • Only 1% of AI-native companies are still in pre-launch, contrasting with 11% of AI-enabled companies.

Agentic workflows are prevalent:

  • Agentic workflows and the application layer are the most common types of AI products being built across both AI-native (79%) and AI-enabled (65%) companies.

Model Usage:

  • Most companies building AI applications use third-party AI APIs (80%).
  • However, a larger proportion of high-growth companies (77%) are also fine-tuning existing foundation models and developing proprietary models from scratch (54%).

Prioritization for Foundational Models:

  • When choosing foundational models for customer-facing use cases, companies prioritize model accuracy (74%) above all other factors.
  • Cost has become a much higher consideration this year, reflecting commoditization in the model layer.
  • For internal use cases, cost (72%) and privacy (74%) are the most important considerations, followed by accuracy (50%).

Multi-Model Approach:

  • OpenAI's GPT models remain the most popular (95%), but many companies are increasingly adopting a multi-model approach (average 2.8 models per respondent) to optimize for various use cases, performance, cost, and customer requirements.

Model Training Techniques:

  • Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (66%) and fine-tuning (68%) are the most common model training techniques.
  • High-growth companies tend to use a greater variety of prompt-based techniques like few-shot (49%) and zero-shot learning (25%).

AI Infrastructure:

  • Most companies rely on fully cloud-based solutions (68%) and external AI API providers (64%) for training and inference, minimizing upfront capital outlay and maximizing speed-to-market.
  • Only a small percentage use hybrid (23%) or fully on-premise infrastructure (10%).

Deployment Challenges:

  • Top challenges for deploying models include hallucinations (39%), explainability/trust (38%), and proving ROI (34%).
  • For internal use cases, strategic challenges like finding the right use cases (46%) and proving ROI (42%) are more significant than technical ones.

Performance Monitoring:

  • As AI products scale, performance monitoring becomes crucial.
  • A significant percentage of scaled products (General Availability and Scaling stages) offer advanced (59%, 64%) or fully automated (31%, 44%) performance monitoring.

Agentic Workflows Adoption:

  • A significant number of companies are evaluating agentic workflows, with high-growth AI companies (47%) more actively deploying AI agents in production compared to other companies (23%).

2. Go-to-Market Strategy & Compliance

AI Feature Integration:

  • For AI-enabled companies, 20-35% of their product roadmap focuses on AI-driven features, with high-growth companies dedicating closer to 30-45%.

Pricing Models:

  • Many companies use a hybrid pricing model (38%), combining subscription/plan-based pricing with usage-based or outcome-based pricing.
  • Currently, most AI-enabled companies include AI features as part of a premium tier product (40%) or at no extra cost (33%).

Future Pricing Changes:

  • While 40% of companies have no plans to change pricing, 37% are exploring new pricing models based on consumption, ROI, and usage tiers.
  • This shift is expected as companies build telemetry on AI usage and ROI to avoid margin compression.

Explainability & Transparency:

  • As AI products scale, providing detailed model transparency reports or basic insights on how AI influences outcomes becomes more critical.
  • For products in General Availability or Scaling, a higher percentage offer basic insights (58%, 47%) or detailed reports (17%, 25%).

Compliance and Governance:

  • Most companies have guardrails around AI ethics and governance policies (47% formal policies).
  • The majority (66%) use human-in-the-loop oversight to ensure AI fairness and safety.

3. People & Talent

Dedicated AI Leadership:

  • Many companies, especially those reaching $100M+ in revenue, have dedicated AI leadership (e.g., Chief AI Officers, Head of ML) due to increasing operational complexity.

AI-Specific Roles:

  • Most companies have dedicated AI/ML engineers (88%), data scientists (72%), and AI product managers (54%).
  • AI/ML engineers take the longest time to hire (70 days on average).

Pace of Hiring:

  • There's an even split in sentiment regarding hiring pace.
  • For those not hiring fast enough, the primary constraint is a lack of qualified candidates (60%), followed by cost constraints (35%).

Engineering Team Focus:

  • Companies plan to have 20-30% of their engineering team focused on AI by 2025, with high-growth companies dedicating a higher proportion (37% by 2026).

4. Cost Management & ROI

AI Development Spend:

  • Companies are allocating, on average, 10-20% of their total R&D budget to AI development, with most planning to increase this spend in 2025.

Budget Allocation Shift:

  • As AI products scale, AI talent costs tend to decrease as a proportion of total spend, while infrastructure and compute costs tend to increase.

Challenging Infrastructure Costs:

  • API usage fees (70%) are cited as the most challenging infrastructure cost to control, indicating unpredictability around variable costs tied to external API consumption.

Cost Optimization:

  • To cut AI infrastructure costs, organizations are exploring open-source models (41%) and optimizing inference efficiency (37%).

Escalating Costs Post-Launch:

  • Inference costs surge post-launch, with high-growth AI companies spending up to 2x more at General Availability (GA) and scaling stages than their peers.
  • Data storage and processing costs also climb steeply from the GA stage onward.

Internal Productivity Budget:

  • Internal AI productivity budgets are expected to nearly double in 2025 across all revenue tiers, representing 1-8% of total revenue.
  • R&D budgets remain the most common source (59%), but headcount budgets are also being used.

Tracking ROI:

  • Most companies are measuring productivity improvements (75%) and cost savings (51%) from internal AI use.

5. Internal Productivity & Operations

AI Access and Usage:

  • While around 70% of employees have access to AI tools for internal productivity, only about 50% are using them on an ongoing basis.
  • Adoption is more difficult in mature enterprises ($1B+ revenue).

Number of Use Cases:

  • Companies typically explore multiple GenAI use cases.
  • Companies with high employee adoption (greater than 50% active users) use GenAI across 7+ use cases.

Top Use Cases:

  • R&D (77%) and Sales & Marketing (S&M) (65%) use cases lead in popularity.

Impactful Use Cases:

  • Coding assistance (65%) by far outpaces other use cases in terms of tangible impact on productivity, with respondents citing an average productivity gain of 15-30% across GenAI use cases.
  • High-growth companies see an average of 33% of their total code written with AI, compared to 27% for others.

Attitude Towards Adoption:

  • High-growth companies actively experiment with and adopt new AI tools (92%), viewing AI as a strategic lever and moving faster to integrate it across internal workflows.

AI Builder Tech Stack

The report also provides key takeaways regarding the most used tools across various categories:

Model Training & Finetuning:

  • Core deep-learning frameworks like PyTorch and TensorFlow are popular, nearly matched by fully managed or API-driven offerings like AWS SageMaker and OpenAI's fine-tuning service.
  • Hugging Face and Databricks' Mosaic AI Training are gaining traction.

LLM & AI Application Development:

  • LangChain and Hugging Face's toolset are top frameworks, simplifying prompt chaining and interfacing with models.
  • Around 70% of respondents use private or custom LLM APIs.
  • Guardrails and Vercel’s AI SDK are gaining traction for safety checks and rapid deployment.

Monitoring & Observability:

  • Nearly half of teams use existing APM/logging stacks (e.g., Datadog).
  • LangSmith and Weights & Biases have gained traction as ML-native platforms.

Inference Optimization:

  • NVIDIA's TensorRT and Triton Inference Server dominate (over 60% adoption) for GPU-based deployments.
  • ONNX Runtime (18%) and TorchServe (15%) are cross-platform alternatives.

Model Hosting:

  • The majority of teams call model hosts directly via providers like OpenAI and Anthropic.
  • Hyperscalers like AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI have substantial share, especially among later-stage companies.

Model Evaluation:

  • No clear standalone leader exists; nearly 1 in 4 teams use built-in evaluation features from platforms like Vertex or Weights & Biases.
  • LangSmith and Langfuse lead among purpose-built evaluation tools.

Data Processing & Feature Engineering:

  • Classic big data tools like Apache Spark (44%) and Kafka (42%) dominate for ETL and real-time data ingestion.
  • Python's Pandas (41%) remains indispensable for smaller datasets.

Vector Databases:

  • Elastic and Pinecone lead adoption. Redis is popular for leveraging existing in-memory data stores.
  • Open-source solutions like Chroma and Weaviate are gaining ground.

Synthetic Data & Data Augmentation:

  • Over half of teams (52%) build their own tooling. Scale AI (21%) is the clear vendor leader.

Coding Assistance:

  • GitHub Copilot is used by nearly three-quarters of development teams. Cursor is a strong second (50%).

DevOps & MLOps:

  • MLflow (36%) is the frontrunner for experiment tracking and model registry, followed by Weights & Biases (20%).

Product & Design:

  • Figma (87%) is the de-facto standard for UI/UX and product design. Miro (37%) is used for higher-level collaboration.
  • Many are using low/no-code solutions for rapid prototyping.

Internal Productivity Use Cases (Specific Trends):

  • Many teams leverage embedded AI features within existing platforms like Salesforce for sales productivity and Zendesk/Salesforce for customer engagement.
  • Canva is dominant for marketing content.
  • For documentation, teams build on existing wikis or use Notion, with purpose-built AI tools like Glean gaining traction.
  • ServiceNow and Snyk lead for IT & Security.
  • Legal departments use ChatGPT and ad hoc scripts.
  • LinkedIn’s built-in AI features are popular for HR & recruiting.
  • Ramp, Pigment, Basis, and Tabs are used for FP&A automation.
